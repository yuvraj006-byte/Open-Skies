?? 1. Show, Don't Explain (Very Important for Text Games)
Instead of telling the player history like this:
More than ten years ago, strange objects fell from outer space...
Start inside a moment.
Example:
The sky turns red.
Something burns across the clouds.
People around you stop walking.
The first "star" hits the city ten seconds later.
Drop the player directly into events. Let them experience the world instead of reading a summary.

?? 2. Make Choices Meaningful
Text games live or die by choices.
Bad choice design:
* A) Go left
* B) Go right
(Both lead to same result.)
Good choice design:
* A) Save the trapped child (risk demon attack)
* B) Chase the fleeing demon (gain rare loot)
Now the player feels tension.
Ask yourself:
* Does each choice change relationships?
* Does each choice affect future missions?
* Can choices lock or unlock powers?

?? 3. Add Stats (Optional but Powerful)
Text-based games work really well with light RPG systems.
Example stats:
* Strength
* Corruption (from demon power)
* Reputation
* Trust (with allies)
* Willpower
Now choices can say:
[Strength 5 required] Break the door down
[High Corruption] The demon hesitates before you
That makes replayability strong.

?? 4. Use Demon Power as a Temptation Mechanic
This is PERFECT for text games.
Idea:
Every time the player uses demon loot:
* They get stronger
* But corruption increases
At high corruption:
* Dialogue changes
* Allies distrust you
* You unlock darker abilities
* Maybe Lilith can speak to you
This makes the player question their decisions.

?? 5. Let Lilith Talk
In text games, dialogue is powerful.
Instead of hiding her until the end, try:
You feel warmth behind your eyes.
A voice whispers:
"You wear my children like armor... and call yourself human?"
Now she becomes psychological, not just physical.
That's scary.

?? 6. Use Descriptions Smartly
Since there are no visuals, sensory writing matters:
* Smell: burned metal, blood, saltwater
* Sound: distant screaming, cracked concrete
* Touch: heat from meteor fragments
Short, punchy lines work best.

?? 7. Multiple Endings Are Perfect for Text Games
You could have endings like:
* Hero Ending - You defeat Lilith and stay human.
* Corrupted Ending - You replace her.
* Sacrifice Ending - You seal demons but die.
* Betrayal Ending - An ally kills you.
* Hidden Ending - Lilith was not the true enemy.
Replay ability = strength of text games.

?? 8. Structure Suggestion
For your story, a clean structure could be:
Act 1 - Survival
* First demon fight
* Join resistance
Act 2 - Power
* Gain abilities
* Meet Lilith mentally
* Make morally grey choices
Act 3 - Truth
* Learn origin of demons
* Decide how to end it

? 9. Keep Paragraphs Short
Text games feel better with:
* Short lines
* Clean spacing
* Dramatic pauses
Big walls of text feel heavy.

? 10. Biggest Advice for Text-Based Games
Emotion > Lore
Players care more about:
* Who lives
* Who dies
* Who betrays them
* What they become
Not:
* Exact invasion timelines
* Scientific explanations
